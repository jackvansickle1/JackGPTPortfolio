The Rent-to-Own Math That Matters

A low weekly payment can hide a high total price. Use these five numbers to compare an agreement before you sign.

By Jack VanSickle · Updated July 18, 2026 · 6-minute read

Rent-to-own can solve a real problem: you need an appliance, computer, or piece of furniture now, but paying the full cash price today is not practical. The tradeoff is that convenience can be expensive. The Federal Trade Commission advises shoppers to focus on the full contract cost, because a longer term may reduce each payment while increasing the amount paid overall.1

The safest comparison starts with five written numbers: the cash price, payment amount, number of payments, mandatory fees, and early-purchase price. If an ad or salesperson cannot provide them clearly, pause. A small payment is not a price.

1. Calculate the ownership total

Multiply the scheduled payment by the number of payments, then add every required upfront or recurring fee. Use the full term even if you hope to pay early; that shows the maximum scheduled cost of reaching ownership.

Illustrative example
$32 per week × 52 weeks = $1,664
Plus $40 in required fees = $1,704 total

If the same item has an $800 cash price, the scheduled ownership premium is $904. Put another way, the agreement costs about 113% more than buying the item for cash. That percentage is not an interest rate; it is a simple way to make the price difference visible.

2. Get the early-purchase schedule in writing

Some plans advertise a short early-purchase window or a lower payoff amount. Ask for the exact deadline, payoff formula, and steps required to exercise it. Confirm whether taxes, processing fees, or missed payments change the offer. The FTC has warned consumers about promotions that appeared to promise a cash-like price but resulted in much higher charges for some shoppers.2

Do not build a budget around an early payoff that depends on perfect timing unless you are confident the money will be available. Compare both the early-purchase total and the full-term total.

3. Separate required costs from optional add-ons

Delivery, setup, damage waivers, club memberships, and other add-ons can blur the real price. Ask the provider to mark each charge as required or optional. If an add-on is optional, request the price without it. If a damage waiver is required, ask what it covers, its exclusions, and whether existing insurance can satisfy the requirement.

4. Understand what happens after a missed payment

A return may end future rental payments without erasing money already paid. A late or missed payment may also trigger fees, repossession, or a reinstatement process. Before signing, find the contract sections covering late payments, returns, reinstatement, loss or damage, and ownership. Ask one blunt question: “If I have paid for ten months and miss the next payment, exactly what happens to the item and the money already paid?”

5. Compare the agreement with two alternatives

A fair comparison needs real alternatives, not just “buy now” versus “go without.” Price a used or refurbished version, and ask a local retailer whether it offers layaway or installment financing. Then compare total dollars, timing, return rights, credit checks, and the consequences of a missed payment. The FTC notes that credit checks and credit reporting vary by plan, so verify both rather than assuming the contract will build credit.1

Ten-minute contract check

  • Write down the cash price.
  • Multiply payment amount by payment count.
  • Add required fees, taxes, delivery, and recurring charges.
  • Calculate the dollar and percentage difference from cash.
  • Record the written early-purchase amount and deadline.
  • Mark every add-on as required or optional.
  • Read the missed-payment, return, and reinstatement terms.
  • Confirm whether payments are reported to credit bureaus.
  • Compare at least two alternatives on total cost.
  • Keep a copy of the signed agreement and every receipt.

The bottom line: rent-to-own should be a deliberate purchase, not a payment-size decision. If you cannot explain the total cost and exit terms in one minute, the agreement is not yet clear enough to sign.
